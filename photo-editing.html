Summary: Harbinger of Fire is a custom level that I made for a rhythm video game called Project Arrhythmia. I made and animated all the visuals here using the level editor's selection of basic shapes (squares, circles, hexagons, etc.) and text elements. I even made the song myself.

Part 1: Robot city assault

For this level, I wanted to make sure that gameplay came first over the visuals. So, when designing the robots that come to attack you in this section, I designed them after the type of attack that they will unleash. - The beam robots have their design built around a central heavy assault cannon, they fly in and unleash a devastating barrage in tandem with the song before dipping out.

- The AOE (Area of Effect) robots are designed to look like undersea mines to convey their role in exploding. Their explosion is in a cross pattern, which is shown on the surface of their shell. I used them to explode with the intense notes in this part of the song.

- And finally, the melee drones are simple darts with a big buzzsaw attached to the front, their arrow design reflects their speed, and the buzzsaw reflects their lethality. They are used to scrape the edges of the screen whenever the song transitions, or whenever I needed to prevent the player from hiding in the corners. I was having trouble making fun gameplay for the "curve" robot, so I decided to redesign it as a larger machine instead. It "jumpscares" the player before firing a massive laser. The missile pods sticking out of its head were fired from the background a little earlier in the level. If you look closely, you can see it move back into the clouds before this "jumpscare" happens During the time of making this level, Project Arrhythmia's level editor was quite buggy and there were a lot of issues that I ran into during Harbinger of Fire's development. In this case, the layering started messing up and my player could clip underneath the shapes that make up the "beam" robot. By the way, each box that you can see on the timeline represents a SINGLE object, like a triangle or a circle. So in order to create these complex designs, I had to group shapes together to make more complex shapes out of them. Speaking of the level editor, to create these complex animations, each object has 4 channels: Position, Scale, Rotation, and Color. You can create keyframes along these channels to change the state of one of these properties over time. You can set an easing type for each keyframe, to change the way an object will move from one state to the next. You can also group objects to a parent, and those grouped objects will inherit certain changes made to the parent such as the position or rotation. I did a lot of careful planning when making this level. For example, this game only allows NINE possible color channels at any given point, so I need to plan ahead and figure out what colors I'll need to use before I start making things in the editor. (NOTE: There are low-opacity objects that you can place down that allow you to create different color values by stacking these transparent objects on top of one another)
I just think this transition is neat :)

Part 2: The Indomitable Spirit

The Indomitable Spirit is the "miniboss" that you face at the end of the level. It is also the most ambitious thing I've made in Project Arrhythmia's editor.
When designing the indomitable spirit, I initially set out to create a Biblically-accurate angel. However, in an effort to push myself, I wanted to create something even more complicated, as it had become a meme within the community that the only boss designs that people make are levitating faces with two disembodied hands. Because the level editor only gives you a set of basic shapes to work with, I had to plan out what shapes I needed to use to create the Indomitable Spirit's human-like appearance and proportions. While this seems like a lot of objects, apparently people within the game community consider this to be quite "optimized"; that is, it only uses as many objects as it needs , so therefore it saves performance and prevents framerates from dropping further than they need to. This animation was made possible by linking up all the different objects that make up the Indomitable Spirit into groups that make up each segment of the torso, starting at the base. Think of it like a skeletal system, but instead of an actual rig it is made up of chained parent groups. When I rotate the "Tummy" bone, everything connected to it from the Ribcage onwards will be influenced by the rotation. (NOTE: The neck and head are separate from the rest of the body, because for some reason it was having issues when moving with the the body, so I had to animate it independently) Designing and animating the attacks for this sequence was a lot of fun but also a lot of work. I wanted to give the Indomitable Spirit a sense of sheer power, so I made these attacks as over-the-top as possible:

- The Spirit breaks the chains that bind it, detonating the walls and sending sparks flying.

- The Spirit regenerates its amputated limbs, while overflowing with so much energy that lightning pours out of its chest, before striking down at the edges of the screen.

- The Spirit winds up and lands a devastating punch into the center of the screen. This sends the camera flying backwards and the Spirit gives chase.

- The Spirit catches up, grabs the screen and throws it out of the door that kept it sealed inside.

These are just a handful of the attacks that the Indomitable Spirit does. It could've been a lot harder but thanks to my proper "bone" setup I was able to move the rig around with ease.
Keep in mind that all these animations may look a little fast because they had to be in-sync with the music, since Project Arrhythmia is a rhythm game after all.
